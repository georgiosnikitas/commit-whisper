What the metrics show Explanation

The activity picture is the story of a project finding its stride. The monthly commit series climbs from 38 in July to a 96-commit peak in March before settling into the 60–88 band — not a burst-then-abandon pattern, but a team that ramped up and stayed engaged. Two hundred and fourteen active days across the year means work happened on roughly four days in seven: a sustainable, human cadence rather than a crunch.

Message quality is where this history genuinely shines. Eighty-two percent Conventional-Commits adherence means the log is machine-parseable and human-readable — changelogs, releases, and git blame archaeology all benefit. The 16% low-information remainder (the “fix stuff”, “wip” commits) is the small, addressable gap between good and excellent.

The structural signals are where to focus. A 34% direct-to-default share and a recent merge cadence that bounces between 9 and 22 per month suggest the branching discipline is still forming. Paired with churn that concentrates heavily in two files and a bus factor of two for those hotspots, the through-line is clear: the repository is healthy, and the next gain is in spreading both review and knowledge more evenly.

Improvement plan Coaching

This repository is in good shape — the coaching below is about turning a strong B+ into an A, not rescuing anything. The themes are ordered by leverage: tighten the workflow first, then spread knowledge, then polish the message tail.

① Tighten branching discipline

  1. Introduce a lightweight branch-protection rule on the default branch so the 34% direct-to-default share trends toward zero — even a single required review catches most regressions.
  2. Smooth the merge cadence: the swing from 9 to 22 merges per month hints at batchy integration. Aim for smaller, more frequent merges to keep review load and conflict risk low.
  3. Adopt a short-lived-branch convention so stale-branch detection (currently unavailable on the shallow clone) becomes meaningful once full history is enabled.

② Spread knowledge off the hotspots

  1. Pair-review changes to src/render/html/html-renderer.ts and src/analyze/engine.ts — the two heaviest-churn files — to lift the hotspot bus factor from two toward three.
  2. Schedule a small, deliberate refactor of the HTML renderer to reduce its outsized churn share and make the surface easier for more contributors to touch.
  3. Document the analysis-engine invariants so the knowledge concentrated in one author becomes shared, not tribal.

③ Polish the message tail

  1. Add a commit-message lint (commitlint) to nudge the 16% low-information messages toward the Conventional-Commits standard the team already follows 82% of the time.
  2. Encourage a one-line body on fix: commits explaining the “why” — the cheapest archaeology aid there is.

Start with branch protection (highest leverage, lowest cost), then invest a sprint in spreading the hotspot knowledge, and let the message-lint run quietly in the background. Three small moves take this repository from healthy to exemplary.
